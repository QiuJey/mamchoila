Prompt engineering is the skill of knowing how to talk to AI — how to phrase your requests in a way that gets you accurate, useful, and creative responses. As AI tools become part of everyday work and life, the ability to communicate with them effectively is quickly becoming one of the most valuable skills you can have. This course teaches you exactly that: how to move from vague, hit-or-miss prompts to precise, powerful ones that consistently deliver results.

## What You'll Learn

This course takes you from the building blocks of a prompt all the way to advanced strategies and a personal prompt library you can reuse.

<Accordion title="Module 1: What Is a Prompt?">
  Understand what a prompt is, why phrasing matters, and how AI models interpret the instructions you give them. You'll learn the anatomy of an effective prompt — including context, instruction, format, and tone — and run your first hands-on experiments with a live AI tool.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Module 2: Prompt Patterns & Techniques">
  Explore the core techniques used by professionals to get consistent results from AI:
  - **Zero-shot prompting** — asking the AI to complete a task with no examples
  - **Few-shot prompting** — providing a handful of examples to guide the AI's output
  - **Chain-of-thought prompting** — asking the AI to reason step-by-step before answering

  You'll practice each technique and see firsthand how they change the quality of responses.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Module 3: Prompting for Text, Images, and Code">
  Apply your prompting skills across different AI modalities. Learn how to write prompts for text generation, image creation tools like DALL-E and Midjourney, and coding assistants like GitHub Copilot. Each medium has its own best practices — this module covers them all.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Module 4: Advanced Prompting Strategies">
  Go beyond the basics with techniques like role prompting (telling the AI to act as a specific expert), prompt chaining (linking outputs from one prompt into the next), and iterative refinement (improving a prompt through multiple rounds of feedback). These strategies unlock a new level of control and quality.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Module 5: Building a Prompt Library">
  Create your own reusable collection of prompts tailored to your work, goals, and creative projects. By the end of this module, you'll have a personal prompt library you can draw from anytime — your final project is to build and document it.
</Accordion>

## By the End of This Course

After completing Prompt Engineering, you will be able to:

- Explain what prompt engineering is and why it produces better AI outputs
- Apply zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompting techniques
- Write effective prompts for text, images, and code generation
- Use advanced strategies like role prompting and prompt chaining
- Build and maintain a personal library of high-quality, reusable prompts
- Critically evaluate and refine prompts based on the outputs you receive

## Prompt Examples

Seeing the difference between a weak prompt and a strong one is the fastest way to understand why this skill matters. Here are a few beginner-friendly examples.

**Text prompt:**
```
Bad prompt:
"Tell me about dogs"

Good prompt:
"Explain the differences between Labrador Retrievers and Golden Retrievers
in terms of temperament, exercise needs, and suitability for families with
young children. Use a comparison format with one paragraph per category."
```

**Summarization prompt:**
```
Bad prompt:
"Summarize this"

Good prompt:
"Summarize the following article in 3 bullet points. Focus on the key
findings, the practical implications for small business owners, and any
statistics mentioned. Keep each bullet under 30 words."
```

**Creative writing prompt:**
```
Bad prompt:
"Write a story"

Good prompt:
"Write a 200-word short story set in a near-future city where AI handles
all public transportation. The main character is a retired bus driver
experiencing the city on her first day back after ten years away. Use a
reflective, bittersweet tone."
```

<Info>
  You don't need to memorize any of these formats. The goal is to develop an instinct for clarity, specificity, and context — and that comes naturally with practice throughout the course.
</Info>
